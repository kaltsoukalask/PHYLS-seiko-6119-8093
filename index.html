The Brand

About Seiko

Seiko was founded in Tokyo in 1881 by Kintarō Hattori, starting as a small clock repair workshop. By 1892 the company had built its first factory — Seikosha, meaning House of Precision. From Japan's first wristwatch in 1913 to the world's first quartz watch in 1969, Seiko shaped modern watchmaking. One of the few manufacturers that designs and produces most of its components in-house.

The Line

About the Seiko 5

Introduced in 1963, the Seiko 5 was built around five practical principles: automatic winding, day-date display, water resistance, a durable case, and a crown at 4 o'clock for wrist comfort. None of these features was new — what was new was combining all five at this scale and price. It became one of the most widely produced mechanical watch families in history.

Anatomy

About this watch

01 The Dial

Black satin finish — uncommon for the period, when lighter dials were standard. The rectangular hour markers carry a fine textured surface, not smooth, catching light differently at every angle. At each position sits a luminous dot; the hands carry their original lume, still glowing in the dark after fifty years.

The marking JAPAN 6119 – 8100T AD at six o'clock is Seiko's internal code for this exact dial configuration. The subtle tonal shifts across the black surface are patina — decades of light and air made visible. It cannot be reproduced.

02 The Case

Stainless steel, 36 mm diameter, 42 mm lug-to-lug, 11 mm thick. Crown at 4 o'clock — a Seiko design choice for wrist comfort. It does not wind the movement. The acrylic crystal is softer than sapphire but easier to polish if scratched.

The caseback reads Stainless Steel · 6119-8093 · Water Resistant. The serial 175681 TL decodes the production date: first digit (1) = last digit of year, second digit (7) = month. Made in July 1971.

03 The Movement

Caliber 6119C — a self-winding mechanical movement. A rotor turns with wrist motion, winding the mainspring. That energy is released through a gear train and regulated by the balance wheel, oscillating at 21,600 beats per hour. Each beat moves the hands one step forward.

21 synthetic ruby jewels sit at friction points inside the movement — not decorative, functional. They reduce wear and extend the movement's working life. Power reserve is approximately 40 hours. The movement drives a bilingual day-date wheel in English and French. Still fully serviceable today.

Operation

Care &
Daily Use

This watch has been running for 55 years. These notes help you keep it going.

Setting the time
Pull the crown out gently to the time-setting position. Turn the hands clockwise only — moving backwards stresses the calendar mechanism. To confirm AM vs PM, advance through a full 12-hour cycle and watch whether the date changes. If it does, you are passing midnight.
Setting the day and date
This model uses a push-button crown. A long press advances the day — the wheel shows both English and French. Short individual presses advance the date. Always set the day first, then the date.
Starting the watch
The crown does not wind this movement. If stopped, make a few gentle circular motions with your wrist to start it. Worn daily, it runs continuously. Power reserve is approximately 40 hours.
Water exposure
The caseback is marked Water Resistant but that refers to 1971 standards. On a vintage watch, seals age. Keep this watch away from water. The leather strap should also not get wet.
Magnetism
Avoid placing the watch on speakers, laptops, tablets or wireless chargers. If it suddenly gains or loses time rapidly, magnetization is likely the cause — a watchmaker can remove it in minutes.
Daily use & storage
Wipe the case with a soft cloth after wearing. Store away from direct sunlight and strong magnets. Avoid drops. With regular wear and periodic servicing, this watch will run reliably for many years to come.
